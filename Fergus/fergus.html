Using AI to Help Reduce Surgical Complications

Dr. Amin Madani, a general surgeon with the Sprott Department of Surgery
at University Health Network in Toronto (Paul Borkwood/CBC News)

Background

According to the World Health Organization, of the many millions of people who undergo surgical treatment, 25% of patients experience surgical complications, with the crude mortality rate of 0.5-5%.

Although according to Ryan Patrick Jones/CBC News, actions taken by surgeons are not the sole cause of these surgical complications, it is still one of the major causes of these complications.

Dr. Armin Madani, a general surgeon with the Sprott Department of Surgery at the University Health Network(UHN) in Toronto wants to reduce this risk.

When he was researching the techniques and thought processes adopted by world-class surgeons, a group of data and computer scientists proposed using Artificial intelligence (AI) to mimic their minds.

The product of the collaboration is a prototype, created analyzing hours of gallbladder surgeries footages with the annotation from expert for where they would dissect. The algorithm starts to recgonize patterns and develop the ability to identify whether an organ is safe to dissect or not in real-time.

The result is an algorithm with a 93-95% accurcy in recgonizing safe and danagerous area in the liver, gallbladder and hepatocystic triangle, according to Annals of Surgery.

Application

Using a gallbladder surgery as an example, "keyhole incisions" are made around the patient's belly area, with a camera inserted into the abdomen and specialized tool is used to cut away and remove the organ.

During the operation, the technology can either colour areas onto the monitor to show whether the area is safe to cut, or it can uses a heatmap-style to show the level of confidence of the safe area.

Potential

Despit the technology is still in development, and have a limited scope of application, currently only applicable for gallbladder surgeries. Madani belives that it have a greate potential to improve surgery around the world in the future, especially in areas lack of surgery experties.

Dr. Daniel Hashimoto, a surgery instructor at University Hospitals and Case Western Reserve University in Cleveland, who collaborated with Madani on the study, stated that the real promise of the technology is the ability to assist surgeons on understanding what they are preceving during an operation better, acting as a second pair of eyes in the operation room.

Challenges

One of the challenge is the uncertainty on the effectiveness of the technology on enhancing performance. While the prototype is working properly, it is uncertain if the application of this technology will improve performance of surgeons and reduce surgical complications. According to Hashimoto, the only way to answer this question is clinical trial studying adverse event, however these event requires a large number of patient willing to volunteer, hence diffcult to conduct.

Another challenge the technology faces is the data required for training. As according to Frank Rudzicz, computer science professor at the University of Toronto, who is an expert in the application of Artificial Intelligence in health care, modern machine learning is highly dependent on data. In common operation like gallbladder surgery, which is the most common operations, it is relatively easy to obtain a large amount of footage of successful surgeries. For less common surgeries, the difficulty for the researcher to obtain useful videos will increase significantly.

The technology also faces the problem of it being a possible distraction for the surgeons, according to Rudzicz. This is because surgeons need to be highly aware of their patients and the surrounding, with the use of the technology, it may distract the surgeon from observing the surroundings.

Conclusion

While other expert say there are still many challenges to conquer, they do agree with the potential of the technology. And Madani have plans to overcome these challenges.

To tackle the challenge of insufficient data, Madani is in discussion with other experts on the possiblity of creating a global repository for surgical videos, which reduces the diffculty for the researcher to search for appropriate data for model training.

For the challenge of uncertainty in the effectivness, the prototype has been tested in live surgery to make sure it works properly, and now seeking approval from the UHN's ethic board to conduct further research on whether the technology actually improves performance of other surgeons, reducing negative surgical complications.
